## Notes
- В абстракте автор объясняет, что мы делимся знанием с нашими устройствами и они делятся знанием с нами. В этом контексте появляется вопрос о расширенном знании. Но еще и появляется вопрос о сущности знания: является ли оно чем-то, что мы производим или оно является тем, что мы передаем между собой и устройствами.
- Автор говорит, что животным и людям необходимо отслеживать собственное состояние и состояние окружающей среды. Он называет receptive state механизм отслеживания состояний окружающей среды. Для людей такой механизм представлен в убеждениях, которые могут быть правдивы или ложны.
- Рецептивные состояния всегда направлены на получение точной информации.
- Вся идея с рецептивными состояниями о том, что у нас есть среда, в которой мы реализуем эту попытку получения надежной информации. На основании надежной информации мы способны эффективно действовать. Например, животные могут эффективно защищать свою территорию если у них есть убеждение (=рецептивное состояние) о том, что на их территории кто-то есть. Я сказал именно "убеждение", потому что человек так описывает поведение животного в этой ситуации.
- ИИ нарушает основной принцип "базового доверия между людьми", потому что для него не существует возможности сказать, что он не знает ответ на вопрос, зато для него существует возможность сфальсифицировать информацию.
- Основной научный принцип обмена информацией [[Neuromedia, extended knowlage and understanding#^3b0633|цитата]].

## Questions and answers
- Would you know more just by using this technology—what we might call neuromedia? (стр. 299)
	- In one obvious sense, the answer seems to be “yes”. (стр. 300)
	- But as great as it would be to have the dictionary and encyclopedia in your head, so to speak, much of it would be a largely passive increase of information. And one might think that knowledge is supposed to be an achievement—something active for which you can be given credit (both literally and figuratively). If that is how you think about knowledge, you might think that neuromedia by itself really doesn’t increase your knowledge—just your information. (стр. 300)
- Two basic questions:
	- First, to what extent does such technology put pressure on the idea that we might have more than one conception of knowledge (or types of knowledge)? (стр. 300)
	- And second, what is the value of states that fit these conceptions (or types) of knowledge? (стр. 300)
- Would neuromedia allow us to increase our receptive states?
	- it seems pretty clear that it would—or at least, it would have the capacity to do so. (стр. 303)
- Можно ли доверять ИИ?
- would neuromedia make us more responsible believers? (стр. 307)
	- That is, would I (a) hold more beliefs responsibly, (стр. 307)
	- and (b) would I hold the beliefs I have any more responsibly just on the basis of being able to access considerably more sources considerably faster than I am able to access now? (стр. 307)
	- **The replie**: but we can tentatively say that the answer is “yes” if we make an assumption: that those employing neuromedia share epistemic standards with themselves and others. (стр. 307)

## Quotes
> Reflection on this possibility suggests that we should not just concern ourselves with whether our knowledge is extending “out” to our devices; our devices are extending in, and with them, possibly the information that they bring. If so, then the question of whether knowledge is “extended” becomes wrapped up with the question of whether knowing is something we do, or something we can share with, or outsource to, instruments. (стр. 299)

> Yet what the possibility of neuromedia shows is that the issue is no longer just whether our knowledge is extending “out” to our devices; our devices are extending in, and with them, possibly the information that they bring. (стр. 300)

> Rather than asking what knowledge is, I’ll start by asking about the point of particular cognitive capacities. (стр. 300)

> I am not going to try and judge—at least in advance—how these capacities are associated with knowledge. (стр. 300)

> We need mechanisms that supply correct information, period. (стр. 301)

> Receptive states, let’s say, aim to track the organism’s environment, and they are causally connected to the animal’s stimuli and behavior. In human animals, we might call these states beliefs, and say that human beliefs can be true or false, with the true ones often—if sadly, not universally—being useful for satisfying our needs and desires. (стр. 301)

> So the following seems like a reasonable hypotheses: organisms that have receptive mechanisms that stably track their environment will do better at navigating their environment than organisms whose receptive mechanisms only work on Tuesdays and Thursdays. (стр. 301)

> RECEPTIVITY: An informational state is receptive just when it has reliably produced—that is, produced by a mechanism that is grounded or stably attuned to the organism’s environment. (стр. 302)

> Reliability in this sense will be a statistical notion—a mechanism will be more reliable the higher ratio of accurate to inaccurate information it produces over a given time. (стр. 302)

> It seems likely that some informational states will need to be sensitive if we are to explain various kinds of adaptive success. (стр. 303)

> Receptive belief is one that is the result of a mechanism that it stably attuned to the environment, and we might think in certain cases, that stability will be so great as to ensure the sort of tracking that sensitivity requires. (стр. 303)

> So if we were to identify knowledge, or some kind of knowledge, with true receptive states, then technological advances in the speed and manner of information-acquisition could presumably increase what we know. (стр. 304)

> Yet it is fairly obvious that this doesn’t really settle the issue. For receptivity, for all its virtues, is a passive way of acquiring information. (стр. 304)

> We need each other to survive. If we are to coordinate our efforts we must not only acquire information individually, we must also exchange information. (стр. 304)

> In some cases—many cases, in fact—we trade information in situations of where trust has been established to some degree. (стр. 304)

> On the other end of the spectrum are cases where we have a reasonably high degree of trust in the person we are asking for information. (стр. 304)

> We ask them because we think they’ll have a good probability of giving us accurate information. (стр. 305)

> We already have some degree of trust that they’ll give us at least some good information, since (a) we have some reason to think they are from around here (they are walking down the street) and (b) we have no reason to think the person will lie to us. (стр. 305)

> I can’t just look and see the truth in your brain, nor can I just look and see that your informational states have been produced by reliable mechanisms. In such cases, I generally need to be given some publically appreciable evidence—that is, made aware of some fact that I can recognize as a reason to believe that your mechanisms are reliable. (стр. 305)

> One natural thought is that we do so by erecting standards for the public sharing of evidence standards for when to count certain facts as epistemically relevant, or relevant as reasons for belief. Call these standards epistemic principles or norms. And we need a concept that allows us to distinguish people who live up to these principles, meet our epistemic standards, from those who don’t. The thought, in short, is that just as we need people to be ethically responsible (responsible actors) if we are going to live together and share resources, so too we need people to be responsible believers. (стр. 305)

> If our epistemic principles are going to function to help us coordinate, it must be possible for us to recognize when we are living up to them and when we are not. If the standards we are using to evaluate each other’s informational reliability are forever hidden from view, they do us no good whatsoever for purposes of social coordination. (стр. 305)

> Not just to live up to our epistemic principles (i.e. meet the obligations imposed by our epistemic norms) but to be able to articulate, or otherwise publically indicate, that we are doing so. (стр. 306)

> RESPONSIBLE BELIEF: S believes responsibly that p just when S is capable of giving irenic reasons to believe that p that are consistent with epistemic principles or norms that sanction S’s belief that p. (стр. 306)

> Real knowers are potential (irenic) reason-givers. (стр. 306)

> Getting true and accurate information is important, and we can increase our stock of such information by sharing it. To do so, we need responsible belief; as such, those who are responsible are worthy of praise. And that fits with a key intuition about knowledge: it is an achievement. (стр. 306)

> If so, then relative to our own epistemic norms, we may be able to better defend those beliefs by having more information available that is consistent with those beliefs. (стр. 307)

> We can begin to uncover an answer by considering the difference between three states: 
> (1) Having the right answer because of a functioning cognitive capacity.
> (2) Being able to give reasons why that is the right answer.
> (3) Seeing what follows from that answer, how it fits into the bigger picture, and - importantly - recognizing which questions to ask so that more true answers are forthcoming. (стр. 308)

> He had the cognitive abilities, together with a huge amount of information and the ability to draw connections between elements of that information, that allowed him to have know-which as it were. He knew which questions to ask. (стр. 308)

> The real question is what cognitive capacity explains Socrates ability to be in such a state [имеется в виду состояние "умение ставить вопросы"] and how to model it. (стр. 308)

> An obvious and fitting name for the capacity in question is “understanding”. (стр. 308)

> First, the type of understanding we are interested in is epistemic understanding, or understanding that or why something is true, as opposed to semantic understanding, or understanding what something means. (стр. 309)

> These two facts allow us capture the thought that understanding is a matter of degree—the larger and more coherent the set of information one has about apple trees etc., and the greater one’s reflective awareness of the coherent connections between one’s beliefs about those matters, the greater one’s understanding. (стр. 309)

> The more that the beliefs that constitute the grounds for your understanding are true, the greater your understanding. (стр. 309)

> UNDERSTANDING: S understands that p to the degree that S believes that p, that belief coheres with a set of relevant beliefs, that coherence is reflectively accessible to S, those beliefs are true, and S has the capacity to deduce the actual and counterfactual consequences of her belief that p both singly and in conjunction with other possible premises. (стр. 309)

> One’s capacity to ask good questions itself hinges, at least in part, on a simpler (and less overtly verbally orientated) capacity: the capacity for making inferences and drawing out a position’s consequences. (стр. 309)

> Yet on the other hand, there is also a fairly clear worry: that the more we enhance our ability to passively receive information, the more we risk undermining understanding. Understanding itself involves more than passive belief, it involves active inquiry. Implicit recognition of these facts is why we can have mixed reactions to not only neuromedia, but to the technology we already have in our pockets. (стр. 310)

> Our reflections on neuromedia and receptivity indirectly bolster this result: instruments, whether inside or outside the head, can be part of a certain type of cognitive state—one that leads us to truth and has value because of that. (стр. 311)
